Faiyaz's Fables

I love fables for many reasons. I like that they are timeless (so long as the animals don't go extinct). I like that are not preachy. They let the reader choose what they want to learn from them. I like that there is no good or evil in animals: it is just their nature, their valid survival philosophy. I see people the same way. None of us are evil, but our philosophies can resemble some of these animals. If we want to change, we just need to change our philosophies, everything else will follow. I like that fables resonate equally with the wise and old, and the bright and young. And in the end, it's a fun story, with emotion and a healthy acceptance of conflict and adventure.

I often learn from other's interpretations of my own fables. Our interpretations say more about us than the stories themselves. When I see something that upsets me that stems from a systematic issue of human "strategy", I like to think of a story that communicates it in a way that even a child could understand.

If you want to ask me about the meaning of life, I think it's about seeking a positive relationship with the world and the people in it. I like the way Ed Sheeran says it "Love and understanding positivity, but what do I know?". Really, I love talking about it. It's about the journey/conversation not the destination/conclusion.

My Fables

To my mother, who taught me to laugh, love, do what I love, and love the accidents that life throws at me.

To my father, who taught me the value of honesty, intention and imagination, and to always ask why.

To my brother, who taught me to think critically, optimally and skeptically.

To my grandmother, who taught me the power of focus, clarity and discipline.

To my friends, who took spent their hours arguing with my stubborn, naive self, and still be my friends.

And to myself, for actually listening to them and everyone else life shares with me, and trying my best.
